\noindent Please consider our paper, ``Traits predict forest phenological responses to photoperiod more than temperature'' for publication as a full paper in \emph{New Phytologist}. 
\vspace{1.5ex}\\ 
\noindent 
Climate change is altering the environmental cues of growth globally, producing shifts in the timing of life history events (phenology), altering resource use and producing novel species interactions. 
But to date, we have observed considerable variation in communities' responses to environmental change, likely reflecting differences in species assemblages and the effects of other species' traits on the timing and patterns of growth. In plants, decades of research has found consistent relationships between traits and growth strategies under different environments---giving rise to frameworks like the leaf or wood economic spectra \citep{Wright2004, Chave2009}---but rarely have studies included phenological traits. Phenological traits are often challenging to measure and highly variable across species, but are likely to correlate with leaf and plant traits associated with species timing and growth strategies.   
\vspace{1.5ex}\\  % 50 word answers
\noindent \textit{What hypotheses or questions does this work address?}  %emw2026Apr12 -- can you come up with a stronger question? Maybe more are early species acquis. and later species conservative and do relationships vary across large spatial scales?
%dl2026April19: still 4 words too long
To understand the trade-offs between traits and phenology require answering: do phenological cues relate to traits associated with early, acquisitive growth versus late season, conservative strategies, and how consistent are these relationships across spatial scales. We combine trait measurements with large-scale budburst experiments to estimate phenology-trait relationships, while partitioning species- and population-level variation.\vspace{1.5ex}\\ 
\noindent \textit{How does this work advance our current understanding of plant science?}  %emw2026Apr12 -- nice!
By measuring 1428 individuals from forests spanning 6$^{\circ}$ latitude and 55$^{\circ}$ longitude, our results provide the most compelling evidence of trait-phenology relationships across North America. Surprisingly, we found photoperiod to be the main cue linking phenology with broader plant growth strategies, suggesting trade-offs between traits and phenology may limit future climate responses.
\vspace{1.5ex}\\
\noindent \textit{Why is this work important and timely?}

We found that species phenologies partially do align with existing trait frameworks and differences in growth strategies, while also highlighting the importance of additional environmental cues or physiological trade-offs. Through our novel multi-trait approach, we can better predict species’ vulnerability to climate change and more accurately forecast future community responses.
